Historical Roots of Gender Inequality in STEM

        Gender inequality has been prevalent in society as far as history dates back. It is clear that there was some heavy discrimination, the female count of Nobel Prizes related to STEM fields, as an example. In addition, women suffrage movements popped up because of the reason of discrimination and the want for more rights and freedoms. Even with the suffrage movements, there are still many traces of gender inequality, mostly caused some variation of a superiority complex.

        Women are also seen to have protested heavily for their rights and general inequality, as seen through the giant suffrage movement, that resulted in the 19th Amendment which gave women the right to vote. However, the gender inequality in STEM started to become more obvious after the technology boom, since there were just so many more men willing to work in the field compared to women.

        Women were also given less opportunities for education, mostly through societal expectation. The idea was that women should keep the house orderly and neat and that they should learn to be good wives, rather than learn to be educated individuals. All STEM fields require some sort of education, and women could not dive into that field without proper credentials. This led to more and more inequality, to the point where the number of men in the STEM fields clearly outweighed the number of women.
